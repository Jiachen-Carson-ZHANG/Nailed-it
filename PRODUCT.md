# Product

## Register

product

## Users
This product serves two connected user groups in the nail appointment flow.

Consumers browse styles in a social-discovery mindset, compare looks, try on designs virtually, understand estimated price and service time, then book with less back-and-forth.

Merchants use the system as an operational workspace to price designs, manage calendars, review booking details, message customers, and turn completed work into a reusable style catalog that keeps driving future bookings.

## Product Purpose
Nailed-it reduces friction between inspiration and booking for nail services.

It combines AI style recognition, virtual try-on, guided pricing, booking flow, merchant scheduling, and gallery management into one connected system. Success means users can decide faster and book with more confidence, while merchants spend less time repeating manual quote and coordination work and more time converting demand into completed appointments.

## Brand Personality
Pastel, feminine strength, young.

The brand should feel soft-tech rather than sterile-tech: emotionally warm, visually light, socially expressive, and confident without becoming childish. The experience should carry some of the browse-and-save energy of Xiaohongshu and Pinterest, while still feeling like a real booking and operations product rather than a pure inspiration feed.

## Anti-references
Do not make this feel like a generic enterprise dashboard, a dark cyberpunk AI tool, or a blunt beauty e-commerce template.

Avoid heavy black UI, aggressive neon gradients, masculine fintech stiffness, and over-decorated "cute" patterns that weaken trust or operational clarity.

Avoid reducing the social browsing layer into a plain utilitarian form flow, and avoid reducing the merchant side into a lifeless admin shell with no brand warmth.

## Design Principles
1. Reduce conversation before commitment: every screen should remove unnecessary quoting, clarifying, and scheduling back-and-forth.
2. Keep inspiration connected to action: style discovery, try-on, pricing, and booking should feel like one continuous journey instead of separate tools.
3. Balance softness with operational trust: the interface can feel pastel and youthful, but it still needs to support clear decisions, reliable pricing, and calendar confidence.
4. Design for both sides of the marketplace: customer delight and merchant efficiency should reinforce each other rather than compete for priority.
5. Let AI feel assistive, not magical: outputs should increase confidence through clarity, structure, and visible relevance, not vague automation theater.

## Accessibility & Inclusion
Target WCAG AA as the default baseline across landing and product surfaces.

Support reduced motion preferences, preserve readable contrast within pastel palettes, and avoid using color alone to communicate booking states, pricing meaning, or AI confidence.

Core flows should remain understandable for users who scale text, navigate by keyboard, or need clearer visual hierarchy during social browsing and booking decisions.
